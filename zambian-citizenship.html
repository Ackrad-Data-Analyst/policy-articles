Zambia Cannot Keep Being the Solution to Problems Its Neighbours Refuse to Solve

Being Zambian Should Mean Something

Land, dignity, privilege, and protection. Being Zambian should carry the same weight that being Emirati carries in Dubai, the same pride that being American carries at an international border. The endowments are there. The system to protect them is not. That must change.

AS
Ackrad Seth Shimwense
Civil Engineer · Data Analyst · PhD Candidate, Development Studies · San Francisco, USA
"There is a man in Kalingalinga who was born in Lusaka, went to Munali Secondary School, worked his whole life in this country, and cannot get a plot of land in his own neighbourhood because someone with a Congolese accent and a Zambian NRC they bought three years ago from an agent got there first. That is not development. That is the quiet displacement of a nation from within." — This article is about what citizenship should protect and what it currently does not.

Zambia has 752,612 square kilometres of land, most of it arable and almost none of it overcrowded. It holds some of the largest freshwater systems in southern Africa in the Zambezi, Kafue, Luangwa, and Bangweulu basins. It has copper, cobalt, emeralds, manganese, and uranium. Victoria Falls. More than 80 percent of Africa's waterfalls. Political stability that the entire region envies. English as a working language. And a population so young, with a median age of 18.8 years, that the demographic potential of this country has not yet been calculated properly.

If you described this to someone in Bangladesh, Ethiopia, or the DRC, they would think you were describing paradise. And in important ways they would be right.

And yet, in this country, a Zambian citizen who was born here, schooled here, and paid tax here can find themselves outcompeted for land, employment, business opportunity, and basic dignity by people who entered illegally, obtained documents fraudulently, and face no meaningful consequence for any of it.

This article is about making that stop. It argues for a Zambia where citizenship is a protected, prestigious, and materially meaningful identity. It gives credit where it is genuinely due to the progress being made. And it makes the case that the work is not finished and that we must say so clearly.

Chapter One

The Country Everyone Wants to Enter and Nobody Wants to Protect

Zambia is not a poor country in the way that the international development vocabulary tends to suggest. It is a country with extraordinary natural wealth that has not yet built the governance systems to convert that wealth into citizen welfare at the scale the resources justify.

The distinction matters because it changes the conversation from charity to sovereignty. From aid dependency to resource management. From asking what donors can do for Zambia to asking what Zambia can do for its own people given what it already has.

What it has is enough to make Zambian citizenship one of the most valuable identities on the African continent if it were properly defined, protected, and resourced. The land alone makes a Zambian citizen one of the most land-rich people in the world per capita. The minerals beneath that land are driving some of the most important global supply chains of the 21st century as cobalt and copper become central to electric vehicles, battery technology, and the green energy transition. The water systems feeding the country can sustain agriculture for multiple generations without the desalination plants and irrigation infrastructure that wealthier countries spend billions constructing.

None of this is accidental. None of it can be replaced if it is mismanaged or given away through weak governance, fraudulent documentation, or a system that cannot tell the difference between a Zambian citizen and someone who paid K500 to a man in Chipata Compound.

Chapter Two

How Many of Those 22 Million Are Actually Zambian

The official population figure says Zambia has approximately 22 million people. It appears in every government document, every development report, every political speech. But if you look at the documented scale of document fraud, the volume of undocumented arrivals that immigration operations catch every week, and the years of systemic failure that have allowed foreign nationals to obtain Zambian NRCs and passports, a serious analytical question emerges. How many of those 22 million are genuine Zambian citizens?

The IOM documented 170,200 officially registered immigrants living in Zambia in 2019, with the DRC as the largest single source.[1] By 2024, UNHCR had 106,770 registered refugees and asylum seekers in Zambia alone, with new arrivals coming primarily from the DRC throughout the year.[2] These are only the people who showed up at a registration desk. They say nothing about those who entered through bush paths, arrived in the back of trucks on the Great North Road, or obtained Zambian NRCs through agent networks and are now counted as Zambian citizens in the official census.

In a single Lusaka operation on July 4, 2025, 255 people were arrested. Of the 213 who remained in custody, 133 were Burundian nationals, 44 Congolese, 13 Rwandese, 12 Tanzanian, 2 Somali, and 1 each Nigerian and Indian. Seven were claiming Zambian citizenship.[3] That was one operation, in one city, on one day. The Africa Center documents that along the southern migration corridor passing through Zambia, 85 percent of irregular migrants are Ethiopian and 14 percent Somali, most of them male, heading ultimately to South Africa but stopping and settling in Zambia along the way.[4]

These are not people who chose Zambia as their final destination. They came because their governments gave them no better option. But Zambia's schools, clinics, water systems, food supply, housing stock, and employment market absorb the weight of their presence whether the official statistics capture it or not.

Chapter Three

You Can Buy a Zambian Identity for K500 and a Phone Call to Chipata Compound

In August 2025, Zambia's Department of Immigration arrested a six-person document fraud syndicate. Among those arrested: a Burundian refugee found with a complete Zambian identity package including NRC and passport in the name of a Zambian man. An Assistant Registration Officer based at the Ndola Passport Office was directly linked to the supply of authentic materials. Recovered from the operation: 903 genuine laminating pouches, blank NRC cards, 15 green NRCs, photocopied identity documents, academic certificates, and institutional stamps used in fraudulent visa applications to foreign embassies in Lusaka.[5]

Chapter Four

What an Emirati Feels When They Wake Up in the Morning That a Zambian Does Not

The United Arab Emirates has roughly 9.9 million people. Fewer than one million are Emirati citizens. The rest, 90 percent of the population, are foreign nationals. The UAE is one of the most globalised, most foreigner-dependent economies on earth. Construction workers from South Asia, executives from Europe and North America, domestic workers from the Philippines and East Africa, entrepreneurs from across the world. All of them are there. All of them are served by Emirati systems.

And not one of them is confused about whose country it is.

Every person who has spent time in the UAE comments on the same thing. There is a social clarity to the place. Citizens occupy a different tier. Not because of rudeness or hostility. Because of explicit, visible, consistently delivered government investment in citizen welfare that every non-citizen can observe but none can access. The foreigner earns well, lives comfortably, is treated with courtesy, and knows their place in the economy relative to the citizen. That clarity is not oppression. It is the natural result of a government that has decided, in policy and in budget, that its primary obligation is to its own people.

Free land and a government-built home as a citizen's right
Emirati citizens who do not own a home receive free land allocation and housing construction support through the Sheikh Zayed Housing Programme. Newlywed couples receive priority placement. A housing allowance is paid monthly while they wait. No foreign national, regardless of how long they have lived in the UAE or how much they earn, receives any of this.

Citizenship access
No application form exists. You are nominated or you are not.
There is no process by which a foreigner applies for UAE citizenship. It is granted only through nomination by Rulers' Courts, Crown Princes' Courts, or the Cabinet, for scientists, doctors, investors, and artists of exceptional standing. The minimum legal residence before being considered is 30 years. Children born in the UAE to foreign parents have no automatic citizenship regardless of how long the family has lived there. This clarity is not coincidence. It was designed.

The consequences are real, digital, consistent, and widely understood before anyone tries anything
In the UAE, Emiratisation quota breaches are automatically recorded and penalised through the Ministry of Human Resources digital platform. No phone call to the right person. No waiting for an inspector who never comes. The system works because it is staffed, digitally integrated, and politically backed. A foreign employer who mistreats an Emirati employee faces consequences that every other employer in the country watches and learns from. That is what enforcement looks like when it is real.

The result of all of this is something that no policy document can fully capture. It is a social atmosphere where the citizen feels seen by their government. Where the foreigner, however wealthy and however long they have been there, knows their position in the economy and acts accordingly. Not out of fear. Out of clarity produced by a system that consistently delivers on its promise to its own people. Emirati citizens love their leaders not because their leaders are necessarily saints, but because those leaders have built a system in which being Emirati is materially, visibly, and daily better than not being Emirati. That feeling of being taken care of by your state is the foundation of national loyalty. And it is the foundation Zambia has been building on without quite finishing.

Chapter Five

What Zambia Is Already Building That Too Many People Refuse to See

It is important, before any further criticism, to say something that is often drowned out by political noise and tribal opposition politics. Zambia is building. Genuinely, measurably, and at a pace that most comparable economies would be proud of. The UPND government that came to power in August 2021 after a decade of governance failure and fiscal wreckage inherited a country with debt at 120 percent of GDP, a negative growth rate, a mining sector in collapse, and millions of civil servants going without pay.[6] What has been built since then deserves to be stated plainly and supported with numbers.

These are not small achievements. They are the foundation of a country that is trying to build something real. And yet the same citizens who benefit from free education, from the CDF, from the farming input support, from the reopened mines and the rebuilt roads, often cannot name what their government has done because tribal opposition politics, social media noise, and the genuine frustrations of daily hardship obscure the signal with noise.

The argument here is not that everything is fine. Far from it. Inflation has been painful. The kwacha exchange rate has been a burden on families. Corruption remains rampant and nepotism continues to cost talented young Zambians the opportunities they deserve. The border between progress and politics is blurry enough that genuine achievement gets denied by opponents who would rather the country failed than credit a rival party.

But the foundation being built is real. The question is what sits on top of it. And what must sit on top of it is a system that makes Zambian citizenship worth something, that enforces the laws that already exist, and that stops treating the country as an open resource that anyone arriving with a good agent and K500 can access on equal terms with someone whose grandfather farmed this soil.

India, a developing country by every standard measure, earns approximately eight to nine billion dollars annually from medical tourism.[7] African presidents, ministers, and politicians fly to India for treatment without embarrassment. Indian universities attract students from across Sub-Saharan Africa. The country has turned its healthcare and educational systems into an export revenue stream while simultaneously serving its own people. Zambia has the peace, the land, the English language, and now the infrastructure foundations to build something comparable. University education for regional students. Medical facilities serving southern African patients who currently fly to Johannesburg or Mumbai. Tourism tied to the waterfalls that Wodemaya described as inaccessible by anything except a chartered helicopter. All of this is forex. All of this is sovereignty through service excellence. None of it requires giving away the country.

Chapter Six

A Law Nobody Enforces Is Not a Law. It Is a Suggestion on Official Letterhead.

Zambia has strong laws on paper. The Immigration and Deportation Act, the Employment Code Act, and the Zambia Development Agency Act all contain provisions designed to protect Zambian workers and ensure that foreign nationals contribute to the economy rather than extract from it. Employment permits require demonstrated unavailability of qualified Zambians. Companies with foreign investment must have Zambianisation plans. Reserved sectors for Zambian citizens are defined in law.

The gap between that framework and what happens daily is where Zambian citizens pay the price. The UAE does not necessarily have more severe laws than Zambia on paper. What it has is an enforcement infrastructure that is staffed, resourced, politically backed, and digitally integrated so that the law feels real to every employer and every worker in the economy.

The ILO recommends one labour inspector per 10,000 workers in developing economies. At Zambia's current ratio, the vast majority of employment law violations are never seen, never investigated, and never prosecuted.

When Emiratisation quota breaches are recorded automatically by the Ministry of Human Resources digital system, companies do not wait for an inspector. They comply in advance because the consequence is certain and automatic. That certainty changes behaviour before any violation happens. Every foreign employer in the UAE operates with an understanding that the cost of non-compliance with citizen employment obligations is real, traceable, and unavoidable. That understanding is what makes the law work. Not the text of the law. The credibility of its enforcement.

In Zambia, a foreign employer who does not meet employment permit conditions knows that the inspectorate is stretched, that the complaint process is slow, and that a well-placed payment or a delay request to the right office can make most problems disappear. A Zambian employee who has been mistreated knows the same. That knowledge changes behaviour on both sides in ways that no law written in Parliament alone can fix.

Chapter Seven

Zambia Cannot Keep Being the Solution to Problems Its Neighbours Refuse to Solve

Let us be direct about something that is rarely said clearly in diplomatic circles or development reports. The majority of people arriving in Zambia without documentation are not here because they chose Zambia. They are here because the governments of their countries have failed them so badly, so completely, and for so long that they had no real choice but to leave.

The DRC has been the single largest source of immigrants into Zambia for decades. It sits on more minerals than perhaps any country on earth: copper, cobalt, coltan, cassiterite, gold, diamonds, timber. Those resources are being extracted at scale, right now, by networks of international commodity traders, Chinese and European mining companies, and local political elites who have converted the state into a personal revenue stream. The profits leave the country. The population remains poor, the infrastructure is destroyed, and the conflict that is partly financed by the same commodity chains is ongoing. Hundreds of thousands of Congolese have crossed into North-Western Province, Luapula, and the Copperbelt, not because they wanted to become Zambian but because their own country has been turned into a place where life is genuinely not safe.

Zimbabwe has a similar story. Economic collapse driven by governance failure, currency destruction, and the chaos of land reform produced one of the largest migration outflows on the African continent. Most Zimbabweans did not choose to leave. They left because their government gave them no viable alternative. They entered through Chirundu and Livingstone into Southern Province and Zambia received them, not because Zambia invited them but because Zambia was the first stable country across the border.

Burundi, Ethiopia, Somalia, Eritrea, Rwanda in certain periods. The pattern is consistent across every major source of undocumented arrivals in Zambia. Governments that have failed their citizens. Resources that are being plundered by political elites or extracted by foreign corporations with the blessing of those elites. Populations that had no viable option except to move. And Zambia sitting at the crossroads, absorbing all of it.

This absorbing costs real money. It costs hospital beds in Lusaka. It costs places in already overcrowded classrooms in Ndola. It costs pressure on water systems, food security, and housing markets in every urban area where undocumented populations have settled. It costs the integrity of Zambia's electoral roll when people who obtained NRCs fraudulently vote in Zambian elections. And it costs Zambian youth their fair shot at jobs in sectors where they are supposed to be protected, when foreign workers without valid permits are employed instead.

Zambia is not obligated to be the social infrastructure for every failed state in the region. Zambia is not the DRC government. Zambia is not the Zimbabwean state. The duty to take care of Congolese citizens belongs to the Congolese government. The obligation to build schools for Zimbabwean children belongs to the Zimbabwean state. These are not Zambia's problems to solve, and continuing to treat them as such while those governments continue without accountability is not generosity. It is the transfer of the burden of governance failure from the governments responsible for it onto the Zambian taxpayer.

"African unity is a goal worth fighting for. But unity cannot mean that Zambia absorbs the instability, the population overflow, and the fiscal burden of every neighbouring government that has decided it prefers plundering its own resources to governing its own people."

— The argument is not against neighbours. It is against absorbing their governments' failures without conditions or reciprocal accountability.

The EU has free movement between its member states and strict external border control for everyone else. The GCC provides free movement for citizens of six nations while running some of the world's most controlled immigration systems for non-members. The United States enforces its border as a statement of national identity and sovereignty, with the understanding that citizenship is a protected status that comes with obligations as much as privileges. While Americans debate the methods, nobody debates the principle that the country belongs to its citizens and that being American means something specific and protected.

What Zambia should be pushing for at the AU, SADC, and in bilateral diplomacy is a regional integration framework with conditions. Free movement for citizens of countries that meet governance, anti-corruption, and rule of law benchmarks. Real consequences for governments whose policy failures produce mass undocumented migration into neighbouring states. Shared responsibility for the costs of absorbing displaced populations. And a clear expectation that integration must be reciprocal: Zambia opens its market, its infrastructure, and its opportunities to regional citizens who come through proper channels, and in return, those countries' governments take responsibility for building the conditions at home that make migration a choice rather than a necessity.

Chapter Eight

Zambian Citizenship Could Be Worth More Than Gold If We Chose to Treat It That Way

Consider what a protected, prestigious, and materially meaningful Zambian citizenship could look like if the current trajectory of building continues and is complemented by systems that actually enforce what the laws say.

A citizen who owns land by right, with a clean and digitally verified register that cannot be manipulated by a well-connected agent. A citizen whose child attends a school that is funded and staffed because the government that collected mining royalties from the cobalt under the soil invested a portion of it in the classrooms above it. A citizen who earns a salary premium in their own country's labour market because the state has decided that being Zambian entitles you to priority in sectors where your presence is legally mandated and enforced. A citizen whose NRC is a document that inspires respect at any border in the region because it is biometrically verified, administratively airtight, and genuinely rare in the sense that nobody with K500 and a phone number could buy one.

None of this requires Zambia to close itself to the world. The UAE attracts more foreign direct investment per capita than almost any comparable economy and it does so while maintaining one of the most strictly protected citizenship systems on earth. Rwanda has one of the fastest-growing economies in Africa and one of the most transparent land and governance systems on the continent. Botswana has been prudently managing its mineral wealth for citizen benefit for fifty years. The choice is not between protecting citizens and attracting investment. It is between having a system or not having one.

Chapter Nine

The Reforms That Would Change Everything

REFORM 01
Complete the biometric NRC rollout and integrate it across all government databases
SADC countries including Zambia are working toward biometric identity systems. The task now is integration: every NRC linked to birth records and cross-referenced in real time with PACRA, the land register, and Ministry of Labour employment permits. Rwanda's Irembo did this in two years. Zambia has the capacity. The political will is what needs activating.
REFORM 02
Mandatory beneficial ownership register at PACRA with criminal penalties for nominees
Every company accessing land, reserved sectors, or government contracts must declare natural persons with beneficial ownership above 5 percent. Anonymous nominee shareholding used to bypass Zambian participation requirements should be a criminal offence with mandatory company dissolution, not a civil fine that costs less than the benefit gained.
REFORM 03
Complete the land audit that Parliament called for in 2026
All land allocated to foreign-controlled entities since 2005, compared against investment pledge delivery records. Land where promised factories, farms, and employment were never delivered should face reversion proceedings. The land register must reflect real beneficial ownership, not the name of the Zambian nominee on the PACRA record.
REFORM 04
Citizen employment quotas enforced digitally, not on paper
Sector-specific Zambian employment quotas modelled on Emiratisation, backed by financial penalties enforced through a digital compliance system linked to business licensing and tax registration. Triple the labour inspectorate staffing. Citizens should have visible, documented, enforced priority in reserved sectors. Every employer should know the cost of non-compliance before they decide to ignore it.
REFORM 05
Prosecute and publicly punish the insiders, not just the buyers
Every document fraud syndicate has a Zambian government officer at its centre. Until those officers face dismissal, asset forfeiture, and imprisonment that their colleagues see reported in the news and cannot ignore, the decision to participate remains financially rational. The insider is the weakest point in the system. They must become its most visible deterrent.
REFORM 06
Build Zambia as a regional service hub for education and healthcare
India earns eight billion dollars annually from medical tourism, including from African leaders who fly there for treatment. Zambia has the peace, the English language, and now the infrastructure foundations to attract regional patients and students. This earns forex, builds institutions, and defines Zambian excellence without giving the country away. Citizenship becomes worth something when the country becomes somewhere others want to access.
Chapter Ten

Why We Left, What It Feels Like to Be Somewhere That Works, and What Would Actually Make Us Come Back

I left Zambia. Many of us did. Not because we stopped loving it. Not because we are ashamed of it. We left because the system kept showing us, in small ways and large ones, that our citizenship did not entitle us to anything that a foreigner with money and a good agent could not also have. We watched the same families rotate through ministerial positions and board seats while we were told to wait for a turn that somehow never arrived. We watched corruption reward loyalty and punish competence. We watched good people with real ideas get elected and then either lose those ideas to the system or get destroyed by tribal opposition that would rather the country failed than see a rival succeed.

I have lived and traveled across the world. I have stood at an American immigration counter and felt the weight that comes with being processed as a foreigner in a country where citizenship means something specific and protected. I have been in Dubai and seen how an Emirati moves through their city, how a foreign worker adjusts their posture when a citizen walks into the room, how the whole social ecosystem organises itself around a settled understanding of who the country belongs to. I have seen what it feels like to be in a place where systems actually function, where laws are actually enforced, where a worker who has been wronged has actual recourse that costs the wrongdoer more than it costs the victim.

And I have thought about Zambia every time. About what it would mean if the country applied the same logic to its own people. If a Zambian walking into a government office felt the same security that an American feels at a consulate. If a Zambian employer knew that hiring a foreign worker without a proper permit was going to cost them more than hiring the Zambian alternative. If a young Zambian woman with a degree in engineering could get a plot of land in Lusaka through a clean, transparent, digital process rather than through knowing the right person in the lands ministry.

What would bring us back is not money. Not political speeches. Not another manifesto. A system. A country where citizenship is the most valuable thing you can hold and where that value is protected by institutions that work, laws that are enforced, and a government that invests in its people the way the UAE invests in Emiratis, because those people are the country's greatest asset and the only thing that cannot be exported, mined out, or bought by a foreign company with a better agent.

Zambia is not a poor country. It is a country that has not yet finished building the system that converts its wealth into citizen welfare. The foundation being built since 2021 is real and it deserves acknowledgment even from those who oppose the party that built it. But we are not yet at the point where being Zambian means something the way being Emirati means something, the way being American means something, the way being Botswanan is beginning to mean something.

While the GCC debates the Strait of Hormuz and geopolitical positioning, while Europe argues about industrial policy and AI sovereignty, while Asian economies compete on chip production and biotech, Zambia is still arguing about which tribe should produce the next president. We cannot develop like this. The countries that are winning are the ones where leaders, regardless of their personal enrichment, at least make their citizens feel proud and cared for. Where the person in the street is not worse off than the foreigner working next to them. Where citizenship is an identity worth protecting rather than a document worth selling.

We are right to demand that. And we should keep demanding it loudly until it is true.

"Zambia is not a poor country. It is a country that has not finished building the system that converts its wealth into citizen welfare."

The land is there. The minerals are there. The young population is there. What is missing is the system that makes being Zambian feel like the privilege it should be. That can be built. The foundation is already under construction.
