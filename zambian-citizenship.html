Being Zambian
Should Mean Something

Zambia Cannot Keep Being the Solution to Problems Its Neighbours Refuse to Solve

Zambia is being hollowed out from two directions at once. From outside, by millions of undocumented foreigners whose governments failed them and who enter freely because Zambia's identity and immigration system is broken and corrupt. From inside, because being Zambian stopped meaning anything worth protecting, so Zambians themselves leave to serve royal families in the Gulf and clean hospitals in London. Both problems have the same cause. Both have the same solution. This article makes that argument with data.

Chapter One

First, Understand What Zambia Actually Has. Because Most Zambians Have Forgotten.

Zambia has 752,612 square kilometres of largely arable land. It holds the Zambezi, Kafue, Luangwa, and Bangweulu water systems — freshwater reserves that countries in the Middle East and North Africa would consider a national miracle. It has political stability the entire region envies, English as a working language, and a median population age of 18.8 years, meaning the country's demographic dividend has barely started. If natural endowment alone built nations, Zambia would already be wealthy beyond argument.

Start with the minerals, because the list is longer than most Zambians ever say out loud. Copper — Zambia is one of the world's top copper producers and is sitting on reserves that the global green energy transition is turning into one of the most strategically important commodities on earth. Cobalt — Zambia holds some of the largest cobalt deposits on the continent, essential for every electric vehicle battery being manufactured today. Emeralds — Zambia produces some of the world's finest quality emeralds and accounts for a significant share of global gemstone supply. Manganese, nickel, iron ore, gold, silver, lead, zinc — all present in commercially significant deposits across the Copperbelt, Northwestern Province, and beyond. Uranium — Zambia has documented uranium deposits, placing it among the countries with strategic nuclear fuel potential as the world debates energy diversification. And oil — exploration activities have identified hydrocarbon potential in the Luangwa, Bangweulu, and Zambezi basins, with prospecting licences awarded and seismic surveys completed. Zambia may yet join the ranks of oil-producing nations. That possibility sits in the ground right now, under land that belongs to Zambian citizens by law.

Then the water. The Zambezi, Kafue, Luangwa, Bangweulu, and Luapula river systems feed Zambia's agriculture, power its hydroelectric generation, supply its cities, and give it a food security base that arid countries spend billions trying to replicate through desalination and irrigation infrastructure. Zambia also hosts more than 40 percent of Africa's total waterfall volume. Victoria Falls alone generates hundreds of millions in tourism revenue and has barely been developed to its potential because the roads, hotels, and infrastructure needed to move people from the border to the falls are still being built.

About 94 percent of Zambia's total land area falls under customary tenure — land administered by chiefs and community structures and reserved by law for citizens only.[1] A Zambian citizen can access this land at near-zero cost through community allocation. No foreign national can legally access customary land without presidential consent and chief approval. The law is explicit. The land belongs to Zambians.

And Zambia has something that no amount of oil, copper, or cobalt revenue can buy: a functioning democracy. In 2021, the Zambian people voted out a government that had been looting the treasury, and the transfer of power happened peacefully. No Emirati citizen has ever done that. No citizen of the DRC, Zimbabwe, or Burundi has successfully done that in living memory either. Democratic accountability is a form of national wealth that most of Zambia's neighbours would trade everything for. Zambia already has it and mostly takes it for granted.

And then there are the direct benefits of being Zambian that most Zambians never stop to count. Free education from early childhood through secondary school — 2.3 million children enrolled without paying a single kwacha. Free healthcare at government facilities. Social Cash Transfers paid monthly to 1.3 million households, reaching 5.7 million of the country's most vulnerable people. Government bursaries and scholarships for university education disbursed annually. The Farmer Input Support Programme giving smallholder farmers access to subsidised seed and fertiliser every season. The Constituency Development Fund of K30.8 million per constituency every year, funding schools, clinics, boreholes, and roads in every corner of the country. Rural electrification and off-grid solar installations reaching communities that had never had electricity. And the civil service recruitment that this government has run continuously and at scale — over 30,000 teachers, over 11,000 nurses and health workers, periodic recruitment of police officers, Zambia National Service personnel, Zambia Air Force, Zambia Army, Zambia Correctional Service prison warders, and other uniformed services — thousands of jobs created every year that go directly to Zambian citizens and only to Zambian citizens. These are the benefits of being Zambian. They are real, they are growing, and because the system that should protect them is broken, foreigners with fraudulent NRCs access them too.

If you described all of this — the minerals, the water, the land, the democracy, the stability — to someone in the DRC, in Burundi, in Somalia, or to any of the millions of people who have risked their lives to get into Zambia over the last two decades, they would not understand why any Zambian would feel the need to leave. That gap between what Zambia has and what its citizens feel every day is exactly the problem this article is about.

Chapter Two

The Official Count Says 22 Million Zambians. The Real Number Is Almost Certainly Much Lower.

The government says Zambia has approximately 22 million people. Official immigration records say there were 170,200 registered immigrants in 2019 and 106,770 registered refugees and asylum seekers by 2024.[2] Read those numbers and you might think the foreign population is manageable and documented. That reading is wrong. Badly wrong. And the Department of Immigration's own weekly operations prove it.

In a single joint operation in Lusaka on July 4, 2025, security agencies arrested 255 people. Of the 213 remaining in custody: 133 were Burundian, 44 Congolese, 13 Rwandese, 12 Tanzanian, 2 Somali, 1 Indian, 1 Nigerian, and 7 were claiming to be Zambian citizens.[3] That was one operation in one city on one day. Between May and July 2024, operations in the Copperbelt, Lusaka, Chipata, Serenje, and Nyimba apprehended hundreds more. The Department of Immigration runs operations like these every week.

If hundreds of undocumented people are being caught in routine operations every single week across only a handful of cities, and this has been going on for years, and vast stretches of Zambia's borders are monitored by almost nothing, then the number of undocumented foreign nationals living inside Zambia is not in the hundreds of thousands. It is in the millions. The Africa Center documents that along the southern migration corridor running through Zambia, 85 percent of irregular migrants are Ethiopian and 14 percent Somali — most of them male, most heading toward South Africa, and a significant number settling informally along the way.[4]

Let us do the minimum conservative arithmetic. The Department of Immigration runs documented operations every week. Using only the operations that were publicly reported between 2024 and 2025, the average apprehension rate is conservatively 200 to 400 people per week across all provinces combined. That is roughly 10,000 to 20,000 people apprehended per year — and immigration researchers consistently estimate that for every undocumented migrant caught in any country's enforcement operations, between 5 and 10 are not caught. That gives a minimum operational estimate of 50,000 to 200,000 new undocumented arrivals settling per year, on top of the existing stock. The IOM's own Zambia country profile acknowledges that the true undocumented population is significantly higher than any registered figure. At a conservative 5-to-1 ratio applied across years of consistent enforcement operations, the cumulative undocumented population — separate from the 276,970 officially registered — is almost certainly above 1 million people. It is plausibly 2 million or more. Nobody knows the exact figure because that is what undocumented means. But the arithmetic from the operations data does not support the comfortable official line that Zambia has a manageable, largely documented foreign population.

Chapter Three

K500, a Phone Call, and a Passport Office Insider. That Is All It Takes to Become Zambian.

The border is one entry point. The NRC office is another. And the second is in many ways more dangerous than the first, because a person who crosses a bush path is an illegal immigrant. A person who buys a Zambian NRC through a fraud network becomes, for every administrative purpose in the country, a Zambian citizen. They vote. They access customary land. They register businesses in reserved sectors. They appear in the census. And their children, born in Zambia, grow up as Zambians.

The cost of this fraud is not abstract. Every person who enters Zambia illegally and accesses its healthcare system is a hospital bed, a doctor's hour, and a medication stock that was funded by Zambian taxes for Zambian citizens. Every child born in Zambia to undocumented parents who then claims Zambian citizenship is a school place, a teacher's time, and a future voter added to the roll without the knowledge or consent of the people who built that school. Every foreign national who obtains a fraudulent NRC and then accesses customary land is land taken directly from the Zambian family that should have had it. None of this is migration. It is substitution. Zambian citizens are being quietly substituted out of their own country, one fraudulent NRC at a time.

Chapter Four

Zambia Is Not the Social Safety Net for Governments That Prefer Looting Their Countries to Governing Their People

Most of the people arriving in Zambia without documentation are not here because they are criminals or because they targeted Zambia maliciously. They are here because the governments of their countries failed them so completely, for so long, that leaving was the only rational choice available. That truth deserves acknowledgment. And then it needs to be followed by a harder one.

The DRC has been the single largest source of immigrants and refugees into Zambia for decades. It sits on more mineral wealth than perhaps any country on earth. Copper, cobalt, coltan, cassiterite, gold, diamonds, timber. Those resources are being extracted right now by international commodity trading networks, Chinese and European mining companies, and a Congolese political class that has converted the state into a machine for personal enrichment. The money leaves the country. The infrastructure is destroyed. The conflict that is partly financed by those same commodity chains is ongoing. And the Congolese people — the ones who cannot get into a diplomatic visa queue in Kinshasa — cross into North-Western Province, Luapula, and the Copperbelt because their own country has been turned into a place where survival is genuinely not guaranteed.

Zimbabwe's story is known. Economic collapse, political violence, currency destruction, and systematic looting by an elite that never faced real consequences, produced one of the largest emigration events in Sub-Saharan African history. Most Zimbabweans did not choose to leave. Their government chose for them. They came through Chirundu and Livingstone into Southern Province and Zambia received them because Zambia was the first functional country across the border. Not because Zambia had invited them. Not because Zambia had agreed to carry the burden. But because the burden was thrown at it and nobody refused it loudly enough.

Burundi. Ethiopia. Somalia. Rwanda in certain periods. Eritrea. The pattern is identical every time. A government that prioritises its own enrichment over its people's welfare. Resources plundered and money moved to private accounts in Geneva and Dubai. Citizens who have no viable option except to move. And Zambia at the crossroads, absorbing the overflow of every governance failure in the region because its hospitals, schools, land, and food systems are still more functional than what those citizens left behind.

The Zambian taxpayer is now paying for the healthcare of people whose governments could afford it if they had not stolen the budget. The Zambian education system is absorbing children whose governments built nothing. Zambia's housing markets and food security are being strained by populations whose presence in this country is the direct consequence of leadership failures that Zambia had nothing to do with and was never asked to solve. This is not compassion. This is the transfer of someone else's governance failure onto Zambia's citizens without consent, without compensation, and without end.

"Regional integration is not one country absorbing the sick, the homeless, and the undocumented overflow of every neighbouring government that found looting more convenient than governing. Integration requires reciprocity. You cannot throw your burden at Zambia and call it African unity."

— This is sovereignty. Every functioning country on earth makes this argument. Zambia must make it too.

The EU has free movement among its members and one of the world's most defended external borders. The GCC provides free movement for six member nations while running some of the world's most controlled immigration systems for everyone outside the bloc. The United States enforces its border as a fundamental statement of national sovereignty. Every functioning regional system on earth distinguishes between integration — which is reciprocal and conditional — and invasion, which is what happens when the overflow of failed governance is simply absorbed by a more stable neighbour without conditions.

Chapter Five

What Citizenship Protection Looks Like When a Country Actually Builds the System — Using the UAE as an Honest Reference

To understand what Zambia needs to build, it helps to look at a country that has actually built it. This article uses the UAE as a reference point — not as a political model to copy, but as a concrete example of a government that decided its citizens come first, built enforcement systems to deliver that decision, and delivers the results so consistently that even the 9 million foreign workers who will never be citizens understand exactly where they stand in the economy.

Before using that reference, the full picture of the UAE needs to be stated plainly. The UAE is not a democracy. It is a federation of 7 monarchies. The Al Nahyan family owns Abu Dhabi. The Al Maktoum family owns Dubai. The Al Qasimi family controls Sharjah and Ras Al Khaimah. These 7 ruling families cannot be voted out, cannot be publicly criticised without legal risk, and control the sovereign wealth funds that manage the country's oil revenues.[5] The 9 million foreign workers who built every building in Dubai, who staff every hospital in Abu Dhabi, who keep every service running, have no path to citizenship, no voting rights, no right to organise, and no security of tenure beyond their employment contract. When the contract ends, they leave. The country they helped build never belonged to them in any legal or political sense.

The citizen welfare programme that Zambians observe from the Gulf and envy from abroad — the marriage grants, the housing allocations, the free university education, the salary premium — is funded by oil revenues controlled by 7 families and distributed among approximately 1 million citizens. Delivering housing grants and marriage funds to 1 million people when you control the world's seventh largest proven oil reserves is not a remarkable feat of governance. It is the minimum a family-owned petro-state does to keep its citizens quiet and loyal in a system that gives them no political voice.

Emirati citizens are comfortable. They are not free. A Zambian citizen has a right that no Emirati has ever possessed: the right to vote out a government that fails them. In 2021 Zambian citizens exercised that right and it worked. That right is worth more than any marriage grant signed by a Sheikh who cannot be removed regardless of what he does. Zambia should never trade its democracy for a fuel subsidy.

What Zambia should study from the UAE is not the political structure. It is the enforcement mechanism. The specific thing the UAE got right is this: it built systems that are staffed, digitally integrated, and consistently enforced, so that every employer, every worker, and every resident in the country understands who has priority and what happens when that priority is violated. Here is what those mechanisms look like:

Zambia has 94 percent customary land reserved for citizens. It has democratic rights no Emirati will ever hold. It has mineral wealth that rivals the Gulf's oil. What it does not have is the enforcement infrastructure that makes those legal protections feel real every day. The UAE has 83,600 square kilometres and oil. Zambia has 752,612 square kilometres, copper, cobalt, water, farmland, and a functional democracy. Zambia has more. The question is whether it will finish building the system to protect it.

This is the foundation. It is real. It is incomplete. Corruption remains rampant and it shows up most visibly in the civil servant selling NRCs for K500 and the immigration officer who makes sure a deported Congolese businessman can return three times. Nepotism continues to cost talented Zambians the opportunities their competence deserves. Chapter Seven

We Did Not Leave Because We Hate Zambia. We Left Because Zambia Stopped Making Us Feel Like We Belonged in It.

I left. Many of us did. Not out of shame and not out of a lack of patriotism. We left because the system kept confirming, in small ways and large ones, that our citizenship entitled us to nothing that a foreigner with money and the right agent could not also access. We watched the same political families rotate through ministerial positions and board appointments across decades while people with real ability and real qualifications waited for a turn that never materialised. We watched tribal opposition politics destroy policies that were working simply because a rival had made them. We watched corruption reward loyalty and punish competence so consistently that the rational decision was to take your competence somewhere it would be rewarded.

I have stood at immigration counters in multiple countries and felt what it means to be a citizen in a place where that status means something specific and protected. I have been in the UAE and watched Emirati nationals move through their city with the particular ease of people who have been told, every day, by every system around them, that the country was built for them. And I have watched Zambians in the Gulf look at that ease and feel something complicated — something that looks like admiration from the outside but is actually grief wearing admiration's mask.

Because the Zambian in Dubai knows the truth. They know that the country they are serving is 83,600 square kilometres of desert managed by 7 families who inherited it. They know that they come from a country nine times that size with real water, real soil, real minerals, and a democracy. And they go back to serving those families anyway, because the salary feels like dignity compared to what home offers, and dignity is not something people are willing to sacrifice for patriotism alone.

The Zambians who worship Gulf royals from social media accounts in Lusaka and Kitwe are not stupid. They are grieving. They are watching a system work for a small group of people in a faraway desert and feeling the weight of knowing that their own system, in a country with vastly more natural endowment, refuses to work for them in comparable ways. That grief is the most accurate signal available of exactly what needs to be fixed.

Chapter Eight

Six Specific Things That Would Change the Equation for Zambian Citizens

REFORM 01
Complete the biometric NRC and integrate it across all government systems
Every NRC linked to birth records and cross-referenced in real time with PACRA, the land register, and Ministry of Labour. Rwanda's Irembo national digital services platform achieved full integration within two years of launch. Zambia has the technical capacity. The biometric rollout is underway across SADC. The task now is completing the integration so that a K500 fraudulent NRC cannot open a land access door, a voter registration, or a PACRA business registration undetected.
REFORM 02
Prosecute insiders publicly and make the consequences nationally visible
Every document fraud syndicate has a Zambian government officer at its centre. The August 2025 Ndola arrest was a start. Until such officers face dismissal, asset forfeiture, and imprisonment that their colleagues see reported prominently and cannot look away from, the cost of participating remains lower than the benefit. The civil servant insider is the entire business model. Destroy the supply and the market collapses.
REFORM 03
Complete the land audit Parliament called for in 2026 — with actual reversion
All land allocated to foreign-controlled entities since 2005, compared against investment pledge delivery records. Where promised factories, farms, and employment were never delivered, reversion proceedings must follow. Nominee shareholding used to bypass the 75 percent Zambian participation requirement in PACRA-registered companies must become a criminal offence, not a civil penalty that costs less than the benefit it protects.
REFORM 04
Citizen employment quotas enforced digitally the way the UAE enforces Emiratisation
Sector-specific Zambian employment quotas backed by financial penalties enforced through a digital compliance system linked to business licensing and tax registration. Triple the labour inspectorate staffing. Every employer operating in Zambia should understand, before they make a hiring decision, that putting a foreign national in a role reserved for Zambian citizens will cost them more than hiring the Zambian alternative.
REFORM 05
Push regional integration with conditions, not open borders for failed governance
Zambia should lead at the AU and SADC for a regional mobility framework that ties free movement to governance benchmarks. Countries whose policy failures produce mass undocumented outflows into neighbouring states must contribute to the costs absorbed by receiving countries. Integration must be reciprocal. Zambia opens its market and systems to those who come through proper channels. It closes its borders to the overflow of governments that loot their people and throw the burden at Zambia.
REFORM 06
Build Zambia as a regional destination — make citizenship worth something by making the country worth visiting
India earns over eight billion dollars annually from medical tourism from developing countries, including African leaders who fly there for treatment without embarrassment. Zambia has the peace, the English, the stability, the waterfalls, and now the infrastructure foundations. Regional education hubs, regional healthcare facilities, expanded national parks and tourism infrastructure. Citizenship becomes prestigious when the country becomes somewhere others want to access through proper channels and pay to enter legally.

Zambia is not a poor country. It is a country where the system that should convert its wealth into citizen welfare was never finished. The foundation being built since 2021 is real and it deserves to be protected from the tribal politics and short-term opposition that would rather it fail than succeed under a rival government. What it needs on top of that foundation is the enforcement structure, the identity integrity, and the regional posture that makes being Zambian mean something in the same daily, visible, material way that being Emirati means something in Dubai. Not by copying the UAE's politics. By copying the UAE's commitment to protecting what belongs to its people.

We are right to demand that. And we should say it loudly until it is done.

"Zambia has more land, more water, more minerals, and more democratic freedom than the UAE. What it is missing is the finished system that makes citizens feel that wealth every day."

The foundation is being built. The enforcement structure needs to sit on top of it. The identity integrity needs to be restored. And the country needs to stop absorbing the overflow of its neighbours' governance failures without conditions. That is the argument. That is the solution.
