"A man in a village outside Kabwe owns his land, grows his food, uses solar power, and has not spent a kwacha today. According to the World Bank, he is in extreme poverty. A man buying every meal in a Mumbai slum has, by the same measure, escaped it." — Something is deeply broken in how we measure human welfare.

There is a man in a village outside Kabwe. He wakes up and picks tomatoes and rape from his garden. His children had eggs and milk from the family's chickens and cattle. His wife took a bag of maize to the local chigayo last week and the meal is still in the house. He owns his land. A solar panel charges the phone. He has not spent a single kwacha today.

According to the World Bank, he is in extreme poverty.

There is a man in Mumbai. He wakes up in a tiny room he shares with two people, pays rent, buys breakfast from a vendor outside, squeezes onto a bus for ninety minutes, breathes air that kills people slowly, and earns roughly $3.50 for the day.

According to the World Bank, he has escaped poverty.

Something is deeply broken in how we measure human welfare. And it is not just an academic problem. It directly determines what interest rate Zambia pays when it borrows money to build a road, a hospital, or a dam.

01 · The Origin of the Ruler

Where the $2.15 a day poverty line came from — and what it was never designed to measure

The $2.15 a day threshold was constructed in 1991 by World Bank economists who averaged the poverty lines of the world's poorest countries and converted them using purchasing power parity.[1] The methodology was designed for monetary economies — places where people buy what they consume. It was never designed for Zambia. It was never designed for any society where most of daily life happens outside a cash transaction.

The FAO estimates that smallholder farmers representing over 70 percent of Zambia's agricultural workforce produce the majority of their own food.[2] When your calories do not pass through a market, they are invisible to GDP. When you own your land outright, that asset does not appear in income surveys. When your solar home system was paid off three years ago, your electricity today is free and completely uncounted.

30–50%
of total household welfare in rural Sub-Saharan Africa comes from non-monetary sources — own food production, land ownership, off-grid energy, and community labour exchange. Every bit of it invisible to the dollar poverty line.
Source: Journal of Development Economics, 2016 — welfare measurement in subsistence economies

So we are not just undercounting. We are potentially miscounting welfare by half. And based on that miscounting, the world decides what Zambia is worth — including the interest rate it must pay every time it needs to borrow money to build the infrastructure that would actually lift its people.

02 · Where It Costs Real Money

How a flawed poverty metric flows into credit ratings — and becomes billions in avoidable interest payments

This is where the problem stops being philosophical and starts costing real money.

When the World Bank classifies Zambia using metrics that treat a subsistence household with food, land, livestock, and solar power as poor, that classification influences how international credit rating agencies assess Zambia's economic fundamentals. Those ratings then determine what interest rate Zambia pays every time it borrows to build the infrastructure it needs to develop.

Only four countries on the entire African continent hold investment grade ratings from Moody's, S&P, and Fitch: Botswana, Mauritius, Morocco, and South Africa.[5] The rest are classified as speculative or junk regardless of their actual economic trajectory or structural reforms. Zambia — with copper, cobalt, emeralds, fertile land, political stability, and one of Africa's youngest populations — is junk. The label alone forces every lender to charge more.

The IMF's own surcharge policy makes things worse. Countries already struggling with debt face additional fees that can add up to 3 percentage points on top of regular interest — a punitive mechanism supposedly designed to encourage repayment, but which in practice deepens the very debt distress it claims to address.[6]

"Africa must assert its voice in global financial discussions. We need reforms that acknowledge our unique economic trajectories and support sustainable growth."

— President Hakainde Hichilema, AU Debt Conference, Lomé, Togo, May 2025[7]

President Hichilema came into office inheriting a debt that had ballooned from 32 percent of GDP under his predecessor to 120 percent.[8] He spent years fighting for restructuring through the G20 Common Framework while the same system that created the problem demanded austerity as the price of relief. When Zambia turned to the IMF in 2022 and received a $1.3 billion facility, subsidies on fuel, fertilizer, and seeds were cut as conditions.[5] These were the very subsidies keeping a small farmer's inputs affordable.

The cycle is vicious and it starts at the measurement level. The poverty line misrepresents the economy. The data it generates is unreliable. Credit analysts in London and New York fill the gap with subjective risk perception. The rating goes down. The interest rate goes up. More of the national budget goes to debt service instead of infrastructure. The farmer outside Kabwe still cannot get his groundnuts to Lusaka before they spoil. The poverty line did not just mislabel him. It helped trap him.

03 · The Infrastructure Argument

What actually reduces poverty — and what the data shows about it

Every country that has genuinely and durably reduced poverty over the last fifty years did it the same way. South Korea, China, Vietnam, India. They built roads. They built ports. They built rail lines. They connected farmers to markets, workers to factories, clinics to communities. They did not do it through cash transfers or aid programmes primarily. They did it through infrastructure, financed at reasonable interest rates because their credit ratings reflected their actual trajectory.

The World Bank's own research found that improving a rural road from poor condition to good condition increased smallholder agricultural revenue by 34 percent.[9] Not because the farmer became more productive overnight. Because they could finally get produce to market before it spoiled, and negotiate a price instead of accepting whatever was offered because they were desperate to sell before the road became impassable.

04 · A Better Framework

What we should actually be measuring — and why engineers, not just economists, need to be at this table

As a civil engineer, the conversation I want to have about development in Zambia is not about daily dollar expenditure. It is about what people can reach.

Can the farmer get his groundnuts to a buying point before they spoil? Can the woman in labour reach a clinic before morning? Can the student download course material without travelling forty kilometres to town? Is there a bridge over the river that floods every February and cuts off four thousand people from the rest of the district for three months?

The Oxford Poverty and Human Development Initiative developed the Multidimensional Poverty Index precisely because the dollar line was failing.[10] The MPI measures deprivation across education, health, and living standards. It tells a more honest story. But even the MPI does not measure market access distance, road surface condition, bridge connectivity, agricultural cold storage capacity, or the economic value of food self-sufficiency. These are engineering variables, not economic ones, and they matter enormously.

The two metrics frequently disagree on both the severity and trend direction of poverty. The choice of measurement tool changes the story — and therefore the policy response and the credit rating.

At 2.6 percent, the default rate on infrastructure investments in Africa is among the lowest in the world.[4] African infrastructure projects do not fail at high rates. They are simply financed at punitive interest rates that assume they will — because the agencies rating the countries that build them are using frameworks built for different economies, partially informed by poverty measurements that do not work.

The African Union has now approved the creation of the African Credit Rating Agency, expected to begin operations in 2026.[11] The goal is to reform the methodologies that have historically disadvantaged African economies and reduce the cost of borrowing. This is a necessary step. But the rating agency reform is the downstream fix. The upstream problem starts with how we define and measure poverty in the first place.

05 · The Honest Question

What this argument is actually about

The question is not whether Zambia has poverty. It does. There are real gaps in rural healthcare access, educational quality, sanitation, and the infrastructure that connects communities to opportunity. These deserve serious investment and honest attention.

The question is whether the framework we use to describe, measure, and communicate that poverty is serving Zambians — or whether it is serving the institutional convenience of organisations headquartered in Washington DC and the financial interests of rating agencies headquartered in London and New York.

When a Zambian household with food security, land ownership, solar power, and livestock is classified as poor because it spent less than $2.15 in cash yesterday, while a cash-employed worker in Mumbai who buys every meal, pays rent, breathes dangerous air, and commutes two hours each way is classified as not poor — the metric is not measuring poverty. It is measuring market participation. And market participation is not the same thing as human welfare.

President Hichilema cannot eat democracy. He said so himself, and he was right.[8] But he also cannot build the road to Kabwe at 22.5 percent interest when Germany builds the equivalent road at 2.29 percent. That gap is not the market being rational. It is the market repeating back a story that was written using the wrong words.

The dollar poverty line was invented in 1991. Africa in 2026 deserves better tools — and the people pushing for them, from Lusaka to Lomé to Addis Ababa, are right to be angry.

"Investment delayed is development denied."

Africa's infrastructure default rate is 2.6% — among the lowest in the world. It is being financed as if it were among the highest. That is not economics. That is a story problem.
